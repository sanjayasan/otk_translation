Chapter 314 — Fallout

An emergency cabinet meeting. Ministers rushed in; among them, GPIF CIO **Koji Namiki**.

“How big is the damage?” the Prime Minister demanded.
“At the close—**2.1 trillion yen**.”

“How did the anomaly‑detector not catch it?”
“It was disabled for server maintenance… and no one turned it back on.”
“The approvals?”
“Cut from five to three. They also failed.”
Every belt‑and‑suspender, gone.

“Who bought it?”
“Executed through Golden Gate. We’re tracing the end client.”

Namiki’s phone rang—Nishida. He blanched. “Golden Gate **Korea**. Most likely OTK Company.” Faces around the table turned to stone.

They considered freezing the shares, but there was no legal basis. Doing so would crater confidence in Japan’s markets—and Golden Gate was American. Push too hard and Washington would bristle. Even a freeze wouldn’t help if the buyer had already relayed title across affiliates.

“Even if it was a fat finger,” Okazaki muttered, “how did they hit the moment so perfectly?”

“Investigate the path,” the finance minister said. “Request cooperation from Seoul.”
“Send the ambassador to the Blue House,” Okazaki ordered—then remembered: he himself had recalled the ambassador as a protest.

* * *

After the erroneous fills, Toyota drifted back toward ¥145,000 and closed at ¥141,000. The Nikkei ended down **1.24%**. Europe wobbled. Headlines screamed: *Worst fat finger; pension money vaporized.*

Korean forums lit up.

– Holy crap.
– 2.3 trillion yen—by mistake?
– Seosung minted 110 trillion won in ghost shares, so…
– Can Japan claw it back?
– Legally tough. Usually you negotiate. If a ruthless hedge fund bought it, forget it.

* * *

We were summoned to Golden Gate Korea. Staff were firefighting. Hyun‑joo, exhausted, glared—then took a nicotine gum instead of the cigarette she’d almost lit.

“Explain. Slowly.”

We told her: time priority, size priority, luck; I was literally in the bathroom. “You expect me to believe that?” she said. “That you just… clicked?”
“Whether you believe it or not, that’s what happened,” Taek‑gyu said.

Ellie frowned. “But what about the safeguards?”
“If those worked, there wouldn’t be a ‘fat finger,’” I said.

A line came to mind. “We get into trouble not from what we don’t know, but from what we’re sure of that just ain’t so.”
“Mark Twain,” Ellie said, brightening. “I love him.”

Hyun‑joo massaged her temples. “Japan will not let this go. Pressure to cancel, to return the shares—it’s already coming in.”
“I know,” I said.
“If we don’t return them, we’ll make an enemy of Japan.”
“We already have,” said Taek‑gyu.

What mattered was that ten percent of Toyota sat in our account.

“What happens if they can’t get it back?” I asked.
She stopped reaching for a cigarette and stared. “What are you thinking?”
“There’s a Japanese saying,” I smiled. “If you’re going to drink poison, lick the bowl.”

* * *

Ambassador **Tanokura** slipped quietly back into Seoul and demanded a reversal “for the sake of relations.” Korea’s Prime Minister **Ryu** listened politely.

“We understand your position,” Ryu said. “But OTK Company is an American firm. If you want this reversed… why are you telling us?”
