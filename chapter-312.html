Chapter 312 — Fat Finger, GPIF, and a Plan

We convened a “shareholders’ meeting” of OTK Company—meaning just the two of us.

“Why would we buy Toyota?” I asked.
“Cooperation? Like Geely buying ten percent of Daimler AG,” he said.
“With Toyota?”

Since Karos opened our tech, incumbent automakers had joined forces. Japan, under government guidance, formed the Auto Alliance—Toyota, Honda, Softbox, Sony, Toshiba—everyone with a stake. Toyota was effectively the leader.

Us partnering with Toyota? Nearly zero. And even if we did… “Fourteen point nine seven million shares for fourteen point nine seven million *yen* means one yen a share. That makes no sense.”
“Unless someone sells at one yen by mistake.”

Fat finger—an order‑entry blunder. In finance, a single zero in the wrong place changes the universe. I listed precedents: the 2010 flash crash triggered by a bungled order; 2015 in Japan, where a broker meant to sell 1 share at ¥630,000 and instead offered 630,000 shares at ¥1; the Seosung ghost‑share incident—110 trillion won worth accidentally issued to employees; Hansu Securities’ options fiasco that rocked our market, with Japan’s Supreme Court later ruling that fills were valid and didn’t need to be returned.

No one sells fourteen point nine seven million Toyota shares in one go—unless they *have* them. Who has that many? Exactly one entity: Japan’s public pension, **GPIF**. Under Prime Minister Okazaki, management had funneled more domestic equity to **Nishida Securities**, whose major shareholder, Endomori, traced back to a wartime zaibatsu—and to the First Lady’s family.

Okazaki’s government pushed investment: amended rules to allow higher domestic equity weight, cut corporate taxes, pumped stimulus, and enjoyed a weak yen. The Nikkei vaulted from the 10,000s past 20,000. Returns looked fabulous; public support followed; the opposition’s “favoritism” attack fizzled.

GPIF loaded up on Toyota—index heavyweight, safer for a giant fund. They were said to own over ten percent: about **15.2 million** shares. If a fat finger dumped roughly **14.97 million** at once, that matched the scale.

At Toyota’s current market cap—about 23 trillion yen—a one‑yen dump would vaporize **2.3 trillion yen** of retirees’ money.

“And we’d make 25 trillion won,” Taek‑gyu said, eyes wide.
“Roughly,” I admitted.

Ethics gnawed at me. “It’s legal but… ugly.”
“Someone will buy it if we don’t.”

TV flashed: Japan recalled its ambassador over the court case and the comfort‑women issue. Ministers hurled insults. Far‑right groups marched through Shin‑Ōkubo with portraits of Yoshizawa, screaming that I was a murderer. NHK aired it, nearly unfiltered.

Taek‑gyu jabbed a finger at the screen. “They’re doing *that*. Still worried about ‘morals’?”

I looked at Okazaki’s face. “If they sell cheap, we buy.”

* * *

We couldn’t pre‑place a one‑yen bid; that would look like collusion. We’d have to strike in real time. We watched GPIF’s reported holdings. When Toyota holdings slipped under fifteen million, we knew the moment was close.

Japan’s market has a lunch break (9:00–11:30, 12:30–15:00); Korea’s doesn’t. We watched from open to close, day after day. I pre‑filled the ticket so one Enter would fire the bid.

The position finally dipped below **15 million**. “Any day,” I said.

Days passed. My eyes burned. Twenty minutes before the close, I couldn’t hold it. “Bathroom. Do not blink.”
“Understood,” said Taek‑gyu, raising his finger above Enter.
